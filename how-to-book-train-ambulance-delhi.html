How to Book a Train Ambulance Service in Delhi: Step-by-Step Guide

 

When a loved one needs to travel hundreds of kilometres for treatment, and their condition doesn't allow a flight or a long road trip, a train ambulance is often the safest middle ground. But if you've never arranged one before, the process can feel confusing — starting with the fact that most families don't even know what a "train ambulance" actually is.

 

If you're in Delhi or NCR right now trying to arrange one, here's exactly how the process works, which railway station to book from, what documents you'll need, and how long it takes.

 

What a Train Ambulance Actually Is (and Isn't)

 

This is the part almost nobody explains clearly: a train ambulance is not a government-run medical service, and there's no special "ambulance" category on IRCTC. What actually happens is simpler than it sounds — a private medical transport company books a normal AC berth (usually 2nd AC or 1st AC) on an ordinary passenger train, and then brings its own doctor, paramedic, and portable ICU equipment on board with the patient.

 

In other words, the train itself runs exactly as scheduled for every other passenger. What makes it a "train ambulance" is the private medical team and equipment layered on top of that berth — not any special arrangement with Indian Railways. Typically, four seats are reserved in the same coupe: one for the patient, one for the doctor, one for a paramedic or nurse, and one for a family attendant.

 

Once you know this, the rest of the process makes a lot more sense.

 

Step-by-Step: Booking a Train Ambulance in Delhi

 

Step 1: Call and Share the Patient's Details

 

The process starts with a phone call to a train ambulance provider. Be ready to describe the patient's current condition, the hospital they're in, and where they need to go. Most providers assign a single case manager to your call so you're not repeating the same information to multiple people.

 

Step 2: Medical Assessment — Is Train the Right Choice?

 

Before anything is booked, a reasonable provider will have a doctor review the case. Train ambulances work well for patients who are medically stable but need continuous monitoring — post-surgical cases, stable cardiac patients, stroke recovery, cancer patients in palliative care, and orthopaedic transfers. If the patient is highly unstable or deteriorating quickly, an air ambulance is usually the safer call, even though it costs several times more.

 

Step 3: Choosing the Right Delhi Railway Station

 

This is the step most guides skip — and it matters more than people realise, because Delhi has multiple major stations, each serving different routes:

Step 4: Documents to Keep Ready

 

Having these ready before you call speeds everything up considerably:

 
  • Fitness-to-travel certificate from the treating doctor, confirming the patient is stable enough for rail transport
  • Discharge summary or current treatment summary from the hospital
  • Current prescriptions and medication list
  • ID proof for the patient and accompanying family member
  • Any existing imaging or test reports, if the receiving hospital has requested them
 

Getting a Quote

 

A transparent provider will give you an itemised cost breakdown covering the train ticket/coupe reservation, medical equipment, staff fees, and ambulance transfer at both ends — before you pay anything. Be cautious of anyone who won't put a number on this until after you've committed.

 

Step 6: Hospital Pickup and Station Transfer

 

On the day of travel, a road ambulance collects the patient from their current Delhi hospital and takes them to the chosen railway station. The medical team typically arrives well ahead of departure to set up equipment in the coupe before boarding begins.

 

Step 7: Onboard Care

 

Throughout the journey, the accompanying doctor and paramedic monitor vitals, manage medication schedules, and handle anything that comes up en route. Family members can usually travel in the same coupe.

 

Step 8: Arrival and Handover

 

At the destination station, a second road ambulance is arranged to take the patient directly to the receiving hospital — ideally a genuine bed-to-bed handover, with no gaps where the patient is left without medical supervision.

 

How Long Does Booking Actually Take?

 

This varies by provider, but a realistic range for Delhi is 12 to 24 hours from your first call to a confirmed booking, since it depends on berth availability on the right train. Providers who claim they can confirm in under an hour are usually being optimistic about railway seat availability — treat very short timelines with a healthy dose of scepticism.

 

What Does It Cost From Delhi?

 

Costs depend heavily on distance, the level of medical equipment required, and whether one or two medical staff are needed onboard. As a rough sense of scale, short routes like Delhi–Patna tend to sit at the lower end, while longer routes such as Delhi–Bangalore or Delhi–Vellore cost more due to longer staff engagement and travel time. Frequently Asked Questions

 

Q: Is a train ambulance a government-approved service?

A: No. The train ticket is a standard IRCTC booking. The medical team, equipment, and coordination are arranged privately by the ambulance provider — not by Indian Railways.

 

Q: Which Delhi station should I use if my patient is in Noida or Gurgaon?

A: It depends on the destination city, not just your current location — a good provider will pick the fastest available train and tell you which station that puts you at. Anand Vihar is often convenient for East Delhi and Noida patients heading east, but this isn't a fixed rule.

 

Q: Can a family member travel with the patient?

A: Yes, typically one or two attendants can be booked in the same coupe alongside the patient and medical staff.

 

Q: What if the patient needs to travel from a hospital in Noida or Gurgaon rather than Delhi itself?

A: This doesn't change the process — the road ambulance simply starts its pickup from the NCR hospital instead of a Delhi one, then follows the same route to whichever station suits the destination.

 

Q: How far in advance should I call?

A: As early as possible once the treating doctor has cleared the patient for travel. Even in genuine emergencies, most providers can still arrange something within a day, but earlier calls give you more train and berth options.
